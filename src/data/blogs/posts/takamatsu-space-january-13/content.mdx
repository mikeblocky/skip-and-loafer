It's not about writing it exactly as it happened, but more about thinking of an episode that would evoke a similar emotion.

**Question:**

"The way you write inner thoughts is so skillful, I naturally empathize and end up wanting to cheer for everyone. When you draw the characters' emotional changes or expressions, is there anything you're particularly conscious of?"

**Takamatsu-sensei:**

What I'm conscious of is that each character has a different level of self-awareness. How much they can "see" their own heart varies from person to person. I think everyone differs in how well they can understand their own emotions. For example, Mika-chan is the type who probably thinks things through very logically with words. On the other hand, characters like Shima-kun, or recently Chieri-chan, and even Yuzu-chan, are probably not as good at it. For them, the realization of "Oh, this is how I feel right now" comes with a delay, seeping in much later. Some people can say, "I'm sad for this reason," or "I'm angry for that reason," while others can't. So I try my best to imagine the level of clarity each character would have, and make sure they don't all understand their feelings at the same speed.

**(01:54:05)**

**Question:**

"I love *Skip and Loafer*. Thank you. The way you present the characters and your paneling are wonderful. The scene in the latest volume where they talk about the future while crossing a stream moved me. When you're deciding on things like paneling and composition, is there anything you're conscious of? As an illustrator, my eyes are always drawn to the artwork."

**Takamatsu-sensei:**

Thank you. I'm actually not very confident in my art, so hearing that makes me really happy. For composition, with the nature of this manga, I prioritize readability over doing anything too elaborate. Action manga might have really cool compositions, but I try to make mine as easy to understand as possible, so you know what's happening. I try not to do anything too eccentric. Rather than doing something unusual that stops the reader's eye, I want them to be able to read smoothly without any hitches.

My brain thinks in a very 2D way, so I have trouble coming up with three-dimensional ideas for composition. As for paneling, since it's a paper medium, you have the right and left pages. When you open a spread, I think about which panel is the most important. On a two-page spread, or even a single page, I decide which part I want the reader to focus on, and which part they can skim over. I try to convey that through the size and placement of the panels, and also the amount of detail in the art. I try to make it so you can naturally tell, "This is the most important part here, and I can relax and read this other part more casually."
